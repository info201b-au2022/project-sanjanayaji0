Abstract

Our main question is: How can a developed national energy grid impact the quality of living of its citizens? This question is important since energy production and consumption have become one of the most significant geopolitical factors influencing a nation’s welfare. As we have seen in Ethiopia, for example, the construction of a large dam has allowed the nation to develop at a higher, more efficient pace, raise the quality of life, and export energy at a profit to neighboring countries. To address this question, we will analyze various overlapping data sets and come to a conclusion (s) from those data points.

Key Words: Energy, Quality of Life, Energy production, Consumption

Introduction

Green energies are put forward as the future of all energy; along with this, we are seeing a modern revival of economic colonialism on behalf of the world’s new and old superpowers (China, USA, Russia, EU*, etc.). Energy as a source of power has also brought about curiosity on behalf of less developed nations to gladly take upon foreign investments in exchange for energetic supremacy (Ethiopia, Rwanda, Morocco, etc.). There is then a contrast between western superpowers who aim to export their green energies, eastern superpowers who aim to export their more efficient energies, and the developing countries who just want cheap power. This brings us to our questions; Which form of energy promises the most grid efficiency? Which form of energy consistently delivers improvements in HDI? And what can nations learn from our findings?

Dynamic Paragraph

We aggregated the following data from each country:

  • Human Development Index: a statistic composite index of life expectancy, education, and income.

  • Energy Grid: primary energy consumption in kilowatt hours, which is the unit of energy used to express the amount of produced energy, electricity and heat. It is separated by fossil fuel electricity per capita, nuclear electricity per capita, and renewable electricity per capita for each country.

  • Economics: GDP (Gross Domestic Product) growth in % for each country.

  • Energy Consumption: primary energy consumption per capita in kWh/person which is kilowatt hours.

  • Energy Emissions: CO2 emissions in metric tons per capita.

We calculated five values from the data to summarize our statistics from each data set. Our first value is the global mean of human development index from 2021. Our calculated value was 0.7181244. Human Development value is measured on a scale from 0 to 1, and in our data we are correlating HDI to general quality of life. The average of this value measures the average for the world. This statistic is important as it pertains to a key element of our research question about quality of life. Our next summary value was the change in average GDP (Gross Domestic Product) in each country in the past 30 years. We are using GDP to represent a nation’s economic standing which is also a part of our question regarding quality of life. Our calculated value was 3.637778%. This means that in the past 30 years, GDP growth has increased by about 3.7%. This value is important for our purposes because we will compare this value along with the HDI value with each nation’s energy grid, in order to answer our central question of how a nation’s energy consumption, emissions, and source of energy effects general quality of life. Our third value is the change in energy consumption for the past 30 years. Our calculated value was 2102.827 kilowatt hours per person. This value shows us that there has been a significant increase in global energy consumption. This value is important to us because it helps us examine our questions regarding how human development has improved, as an increase in energy consumption indicates an increase in development. Our fourth value was the change in CO2 emissions per country in the last 30 years. Our calculated value was -0.3878026. This indicates that there has been a decrease in CO2 emissions, which goes along with our background research in which we learned emissions from factories have significantly reduced, though there have been increased use of cars which has increased the overall carbon footprint. This value is pertinent to our question of how energy emissions and consumption is affected by quality of life. Our final value we calculated was the number of kilowatt hours for the country which currenly uses the most renewable energy. Our calculated value was 56769.74 kilowatt hours, and the country was Iceland. This value is important to our data because countries which use more renewable energy are more environmentally sustainable, and have a more developed energy grid. By comparing this value with our quality of life factors, we are able to answer our research questions.
